## Full Findings

### 1. Permanent Establishment Risk for Foreign Companies with US Remote Workers

**The core problem:** When a foreign company has employees or contractors working remotely from the US, it risks creating a "permanent establishment" -- a taxable business presence that subjects the company to US corporate income tax on profits attributable to that presence.

**OECD 2025 Framework (November 2025 update):**
The OECD updated its Model Tax Convention Commentary with the most substantial revisions since 2017, directly addressing remote work PE risk:

- **50% time threshold safe harbor**: If an employee works from a home/remote location less than 50% of total working time over 12 months, that location is generally NOT a place of business (no PE). Source: [Ogletree](https://ogletree.com/insights-resources/blog-posts/cross-border-remote-work-and-permanent-establishment-mitigating-risk-for-multinational-employers/)
- **Commercial reason test**: For arrangements exceeding 50%, the OECD examines whether the employee's presence serves a genuine commercial purpose (local clients, regional markets) vs. personal convenience. Cost-reduction motives (reducing office space) do NOT count as commercial reasons. Source: [KPMG](https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/gms-flash-alert/flash-alert-2025-234.html)
- **No automatic PE from remote work**: Mere employee-driven remote work does not automatically create PE, providing relief for employers managing hybrid teams.

**Critical US-specific caveat:** US tax treaties follow the US Model, not the OECD Model. The IRS and Treasury treat OECD Commentary as non-binding interpretive material. Each US treaty has its own Treasury-issued Technical Explanation. Foreign companies cannot simply rely on the OECD 50% safe harbor when dealing with US tax exposure. Source: [KPMG](https://kpmg.com/xx/en/our-insights/gms-flash-alert/flash-alert-2025-234.html)

**Risk factors ranked by severity:**
1. **Dependent agents**: Employees who habitually conclude contracts or play the principal role in sales create the highest PE risk. Sales teams are "particularly high-risk groups." Source: [icon.partners](https://www.icon.partners/post/permanent-establishment-pe-risk-explained-for-remote-teams)
2. **Fixed place of business**: A home office "at the disposal" of the enterprise used continuously for business can trigger PE.
3. **Revenue-generating activities**: Core business functions (not preparatory/auxiliary) performed on the ground increase PE risk significantly.
4. **Management presence**: If strategic decisions are habitually made in the US, this raises corporate tax residence issues (Place of Effective Management).

**Consequences of triggering PE:**
- Corporate income tax on attributed profits
- Back taxes potentially dating back years
- Heavy interest charges and penalties
- Double taxation risk (may also owe in home country)
- Red flags in M&A/IPO due diligence
- Collateral failures in payroll tax withholding and VAT compliance

Source: [icon.partners](https://www.icon.partners/post/permanent-establishment-pe-risk-explained-for-remote-teams)

**Industry sentiment:** PE has become the "#1 compliance concern" for global mobility leaders, outranking even immigration or data security. Source: [wfa.team](https://wfa.team/blog/remote-work-permanent-establishment-pe-checklist/)

### 2. Multi-State Payroll Tax Exposure

**The fundamental rule:** Payroll compliance follows where the employee physically performs work, not where the company is headquartered.

**Key pain points:**

- **22 states** trigger filing obligations from even a single day of work. Source: [teamed.global](https://www.teamed.global/blog/multi-state-payroll-compliance-changes-in-2026)
- **5 distinct compliance categories** are triggered per new state: state income tax withholding, SUI registration, wage and hour law compliance, state-mandated benefits/leave, and new hire reporting.
- **SUI rates vary dramatically** from 0.1% to over 10% depending on state and experience rating.
- **Only 15 states + DC** have reciprocity agreements that eliminate double withholding. California has NO reciprocity agreements with any state.
- **New York's "convenience of the employer" rule**: If a company is headquartered in New York and an employee works remotely from Florida, New York may still claim the right to tax that income unless the employee works remotely out of necessity. Source: [teamed.global](https://www.teamed.global/blog/multi-state-payroll-compliance-changes-in-2026)
- **Local taxes add another layer**: NYC income tax, Philadelphia wage tax (3.43% for nonresidents), and many Ohio municipalities impose their own income taxes.

**Most common failure patterns:**
1. Assuming HQ state rules apply everywhere
2. Missing local (city/county) taxes
3. Not registering for SUI in the work state
4. Not updating withholding after a mid-year employee move
5. Not documenting remote-work arrangements (no audit trail)

**Operational burden:** A single untracked remote-work move creates 4+ additional compliance touchpoints. Companies with employees in 5+ states should consider remaining on EOR if they have fewer than 5 employees per state. Source: [teamed.global](https://www.teamed.global/blog/multi-state-payroll-compliance-changes-in-2026)

### 3. H-1B / LCA Violations for Remote Workers

**Core requirement:** Every location where an H-1B/E-3/H-1B1 employee performs work must be listed on a Labor Condition Application (LCA) and covered by the underlying petition -- including home offices.

**Within-MSA moves require:**
- Posting notice at the new residence for 10 business days
- Updating the Public Access File
- Common failure: HR updates payroll but fails to notify immigration counsel

**Outside-MSA moves require:**
- Filing an amended H-1B petition with a new LCA *before* work begins at the new location
- New prevailing wage determination for the new geographic area
- Common failure: Employees relocate without the employer knowing

**Case study from Greenberg Traurig:** A software engineer moved from Dallas (prevailing wage ~$157K) to San Francisco (~$213K) without the immigration team being notified. Two years later, USCIS cross-referenced state tax filings and discovered the discrepancy. Result: $113K in back-pay exposure, immediate status risk, jeopardized extension, and company-wide compliance overhaul. Source: [gtlaw](https://www.gtlaw-insidebusinessimmigration.com/immigration-and-compliance/remote-work-compliance-considerations-for-h-1b-e-3-and-h-1b1-employees/)

**Enforcement is intensifying:**
- FDNS conducts unannounced site visits to verify petition information
- USCIS cross-references state tax filings against petition addresses during adjudication
- Biometric RFEs are being used as address verification tools, even for I-140 immigrant petitions
- NTAs (Notices to Appear) are being issued more aggressively when violations are discovered

**Penalties:** Civil monetary penalties up to $8,527 per violation, back wages, potential debarment from H-1B program. Source: [immigrationfleet.com](https://immigrationfleet.com/articles/best-practices-for-lca-posting-and-recordkeeping-in-hybrid-work-environments/)

**New employer risk:** H-1B change-of-employer petitions can expose violations from the *prior* employer. New employers may unknowingly inherit compliance problems they had no role in creating. Source: [gtlaw](https://www.gtlaw-insidebusinessimmigration.com/immigration-and-compliance/remote-work-compliance-considerations-for-h-1b-e-3-and-h-1b1-employees/)

**Papaya Global projects** regulatory-driven contractor conversion will account for $2.5B in EOR revenue by 2030, nearly tripling from 2025 levels. Source: [papayaglobal.com PDF](https://www.papayaglobal.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/02/The-rise-of-Employer-of-Record-EOR-Reshaping-global-workforce-strategy.pdf)

**Key gaps in the EOR/compliance tool landscape:**

1. **No unified compliance stack**: EORs handle payroll and legal employment; global mobility platforms (wfa.team, Topia, Mercer) handle PE risk assessment and travel tracking; immigration platforms are separate systems. No single tool covers payroll + PE risk + immigration compliance + multi-state tax + location tracking.

2. **PE risk assessment is not automated in EORs**: EOR platforms solve the *employment* problem but don't inherently assess whether a remote arrangement creates PE risk for the parent company. That requires separate analysis, usually via global mobility software or external tax advisors.

3. **Real-time location tracking is weak**: Most tools rely on self-reported employee location data. The gap between where employees *say* they work and where they *actually* work is the root cause of most compliance failures (especially H-1B LCA violations and multi-state tax issues).

4. **Immigration-payroll integration is almost nonexistent**: As the Greenberg Traurig case study illustrates, payroll teams and immigration teams operate in silos. An address change in payroll rarely triggers immigration review automatically.

5. **US domestic multi-state compliance is underserved by global platforms**: Most EOR platforms focus on international hiring. US-to-US multi-state complexity (50 states with different tax, labor, leave, and SUI rules) is a different problem that global EORs don't specialize in.

- **Topia**: Enterprise relocation management with scenario planning and cost projections.
- **Mercer**: Deep data insights for policy benchmarking and compensation data.
- **Localyze**: Immigration and relocation platform (appeared in search results but minimal differentiation data).
- **Equus**: AssignmentPro for relocations + PinPoint for workforce tracking.

Source: [wfa.team](https://wfa.team/blog/best-global-mobility-software/)

**Gap**: These platforms focus on cross-border (international) moves. US domestic multi-state compliance tracking is not their primary use case.
